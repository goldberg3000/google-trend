National day of mourning - Wikipedia

A national day of mourning is an official day designated by a country's government to commemorate and mourn significant losses, such as the death of prominent individuals, wartime events, or major disasters. These days often involve collective mourning activities and rituals intended to foster a sense of national unity and shared grief among the populace. A common symbol of such days is the flying of the national or military flag at half-mast. The concept of national mourning is sociologically understood as a means for state authorities to create a communal space for grief, reinforcing a collective identity in the face of loss. Various countries observe these days in response to significant events, and they can be triggered by both domestic and international occurrences. In addition to national observances, similar concepts exist at regional levels, such as the European Union and the Arab League. The European Commission first recognized a day of mourning on September 12, 2001, following the terrorist attacks in the United States, and has since observed additional days for other tragedies. The Arab League has also declared days of mourning in response to significant events. Other international organizations, like the African Union and the Union of South American Nations, have similarly declared days of mourning. While not identical to national days of mourning, some events and protests may also be referred to as a "day of mourning," reflecting a broader cultural practice of remembrance and reflection on loss.
